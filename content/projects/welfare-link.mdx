## The problem

Korea hits the "super-aged society" mark in 2025 — over 20% of the population is 65 or older. The government keeps adding welfare benefits, and almost nobody navigates them alone. They get navigated by adult children translating bureaucracy on weekend phone calls.

Every existing welfare app is built for the elderly user as if they were a single user. Single login, single profile, single recommendation model. The actual decision unit — parent + adult child — has nowhere to live in the product.

## My role

Team Lead of 6 for an HCI/UX Innovation course at Kookmin. I owned product direction from problem definition through final interactive Figma prototype, ran the user research, and split design execution with the rest of the team.

## Key decisions

### Designed for two users at once

User research with 6 participants — 3 elderly users 60+, 3 adult children in their 40s–50s. Most accessibility design assumes the elderly user is alone. Our research said the opposite: every elderly participant talked through welfare decisions with at least one child. The "user" wasn't a person, it was a relationship.

So we designed two views on the same data — a simplified, voice-first view for the parent and a denser, action-oriented view for the child. They sync. The child can pre-fill applications the parent reviews and approves.

### Voice-first over text-first

The default accessibility move is "make the text bigger." The actual blocker was different: Korean welfare bureaucracy uses dense administrative language even at 24px. Reading wasn't the problem; comprehension was.

So we made voice the primary input/output surface. The system reads recommendations aloud in everyday Korean. Voice is also a permission gate — the parent confirms applications by speaking, not tapping.

### AI recommendations around family context, not individual profile

Most recommender systems profile the user. We profiled the household. The child's recent activity — job changes, location, financial stress — shapes what the parent sees, because in practice those are the things that change which welfare programs are useful.

This was the most contentious design call inside the team — privacy-adjacent, requires explicit consent flows. We kept it because every interview confirmed that elderly participants didn't think of welfare as an "individual" thing in the first place.

## What's shipped

- Brand identity, user flows for both user types, fully interactive Figma prototype
- 8+ key screens covering onboarding, recommendation review, application drafting, voice confirmation, and family sync
- Research synthesis from 6 participant interviews across two age cohorts

## What this taught me

That accessibility design fails when it stops at the surface — font size, contrast, big buttons — and skips the social context. The hardest UX problem in elder care isn't sensory. It's that the decision is being made by two people on different devices, in different vocabularies.

That a "team lead" role on a 6-person student team is mostly the work of letting the right disagreement happen. The privacy debate around the household-profile recommender was the team's best discussion of the semester, and the design wouldn't have been as defensible without it.
