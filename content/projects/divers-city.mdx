## The problem

A migrant worker in Korea building a five-year track record — language proficiency, employer references, a clean payment history — loses all of it the moment they cross a border. The next country's employers and government agencies have no way to verify any of it. Every relocation resets to zero.

Most policy responses treat this isolation as an individual problem — build better support services, simplify forms, add another portal. But the structural shape is a network problem. Credentials and reputation need to *move with the worker.*

## Key decisions

### Reframed isolation as a network problem

The default framing for migrant worker support is "more services per person." We pushed back: the binding constraint isn't the quality of services; it's that they don't connect across borders. Once isolation was reframed as a network problem, the solution stopped being "another welfare portal" and became *portable infrastructure for credentials, references, and rights.*

### AI for personalization, blockchain for verification — split by job

Two technologies, two jobs.

- **AI** handles the soft layer: matching workers to opportunities, translating across languages and bureaucratic systems, surfacing rights relevant to a given country and visa status.
- **Blockchain** handles the hard layer: making employer references, language certifications, and payment history verifiable by the next country's actors without trusting Korea's source systems.

The split avoided the common mistake of making AI carry verification (it can't) or making blockchain carry recommendation (it shouldn't). Each layer does what it's actually good at.

### Five modules, three user groups

`career mobility · AI communication · protection & evidence · rights support · policy intelligence`

The modules were designed to serve three groups simultaneously — workers (most direct), employers (verification + retention), and governments (policy intelligence). Designing for three user groups in a single platform forced explicit role-scoped surfaces — every module had to make sense from each perspective independently.

## What this taught me

That the best concept design happens when you let the policy data force a structural reframe. We didn't start with "what should the app do?" — we started with what every existing approach was assuming, and questioned that. The five-module shape fell out of the reframe, not the other way.

That solo-authoring a 16-page proposal is a different muscle than collaborative design. Every paragraph has to defend itself; every visual has to do its own work. It's the closest student-project version of writing a real product spec.
